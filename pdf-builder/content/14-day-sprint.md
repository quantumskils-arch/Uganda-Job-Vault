# Start Here — Read This First

You already have the two most important things: the **PSC Graduate Recruitment Aptitude Pack** and the **URA Aptitude Past Questions**. That is the *what*. This Sprint is the *how* — the order to study them in, what to do each day, and exactly how to behave on exam morning so your preparation actually shows up when it counts.

Most candidates fail aptitude tests not because they are not smart enough, but because they walk in unprepared, anxious, and out of time. They never made a plan. You are not going to be one of them.

This plan assumes you have **14 days** before your test. If you have fewer, do not panic — skip to the box below and compress. If you have more, repeat the practice days.

::: tip How to use this Sprint
Study in short, honest sessions — 60 to 90 minutes a day beats a 6-hour cram once a week. Tick off each day as you finish it. Keep your PSC and URA packs open beside this plan. The goal is not to "finish the material" — it is to walk in *calm and ready*.
:::

::: warn Less than 14 days?
**7 days:** do Days 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 only. **3 days:** do Days 1, 7, and 13 (diagnostic, full mock, exam-day prep). Something is always better than nothing.
:::

# Week 1 — Build the Foundation

The first week is about finding out where you actually stand, then fixing the weak spots one section at a time. Do not rush to "do questions." First understand the test.

@day Day 1 — The Honest Diagnostic
Open your PSC pack. Without studying anything first, attempt **20 questions across all sections** under a timer (give yourself 25 minutes). Mark them honestly. The point is not the score — it is to find your weakest section. Write it down: *"My weakest area is ______."* That section gets the most attention this week. This honesty today saves you on exam day.

@day Day 2 — Numerical Reasoning
Focus only on numerical questions today — percentages, ratios, data tables, simple interest, rates. Work through the numerical section of your PSC pack slowly, reading every explanation even for ones you got right. Speed comes later; understanding comes first.

@day Day 3 — Verbal Reasoning
Today is comprehension, vocabulary, and "true / false / cannot say" statements. The trick with verbal reasoning: answer **only from the passage given**, never from your own outside knowledge. Practise spotting when a statement says more than the passage actually supports.

@day Day 4 — Logical & Abstract Reasoning
Patterns, sequences, odd-one-out, and "what comes next." These reward calm pattern-spotting, not panic. Work through your PSC logical section. When stuck, look at what *changes* between each step — that is almost always the rule.

@day Day 5 — Situational Judgement
The PSC and government tests love these. There is usually a "most appropriate" and "least appropriate" action. The safe instinct: choose the answer that is **professional, honest, follows proper procedure, and protects the public interest** — not the one that is fastest or most personally convenient.

<!-- pagebreak -->

@day Day 6 — URA Deep Dive
Switch to your URA pack today. URA's style leans heavier on numerical and situational questions relevant to revenue and tax work. Do a full section, note how the URA questions differ in tone from the general PSC ones, and get comfortable with the format.

@day Day 7 — First Full Mock
Sit a **full timed mock** using questions you have not seen yet, across all sections, no breaks, no phone. This is the most important day of Week 1. Score it. Compare to Day 1. You will almost always have improved — that proof is what kills the anxiety.

# Week 2 — Sharpen & Speed Up

You know the material now. Week 2 is about getting **faster, more accurate, and calmer under pressure** — and fixing the exact mistakes your Week 1 mock revealed.

@day Day 8 — Review Your Mock
Go back through every question you got wrong on Day 7. For each one, write *why* you missed it: was it speed, a careless slip, or genuinely not knowing? This single habit — reviewing mistakes properly — improves scores faster than doing hundreds of fresh questions.

@day Day 9 — Attack Your Weakest Section
Remember the weak area you named on Day 1? Spend the whole session on it. By now it should feel far less frightening. Do questions until you can explain the *method* out loud, not just guess.

@day Day 10 — Timing Drills
Today is purely about speed. Set a strict time per question (roughly 60–75 seconds) and practise moving on when stuck. **Learn to skip and return** — leaving a hard question to grab three easy marks elsewhere is a skill, not a weakness.

@day Day 11 — Mixed Practice
Do a mixed set pulling from all sections, jumping between question types the way the real test does. This trains your brain to switch gears quickly — the thing that catches people out on the day.

@day Day 12 — Second Full Mock
Sit your second full timed mock. Treat it like the real thing: same time of day if you can, no interruptions. Score it. The gap between this and your Day 1 diagnostic is the proof of how far you have come.

@day Day 13 — Light Review & Logistics
Go light today — review your mistake notes, redo a handful of tricky questions, then **stop early.** Sort out the practical things: confirm your test venue and time, prepare your documents and IDs, plan your route and how early you will leave. Cramming now hurts more than it helps.

@day Day 14 — Rest & Reset
The day before the test, **do almost no studying.** A short, light review in the morning at most. Eat well, sleep early, lay out everything you need. Your brain consolidates what you learned while you rest. Trust the work you have already done.

# The Exam-Day Playbook

This is the part most guides skip — and the part that wins or loses marks. Read it the night before and the morning of.

::: tip The night before
Lay out your documents, IDs, pens, and admission details. Set two alarms. Decide what you will eat for breakfast. Go to bed early — sleep is part of your preparation, not separate from it.
:::

## The morning of

Eat a real breakfast — your brain runs on glucose, and a tested-tired brain makes careless mistakes. Leave **earlier than you think you need to**; arriving rushed and panicked costs you marks before you even start. Aim to be at the venue with time to settle and breathe.

## In the first 60 seconds

When the test starts, do not dive in. Take three slow breaths. Read the instructions properly — how many questions, how much time, is there negative marking. Work out roughly how many minutes you have per question. This 60 seconds of calm planning is worth more than 60 seconds of frantic answering.

## How to manage your time

::: note The golden rule of aptitude tests
**Easy marks first.** Go through and answer every question you find easy or quick. Then return for the harder ones with whatever time remains. Never let one hard question eat the time of five easy ones.
:::

If a question is taking too long, **mark it, guess if there is no penalty, and move on.** You can come back. The candidate who finishes calmly with a few guesses usually beats the one who runs out of time stuck on question 12.

## If panic rises

It happens to everyone. If your mind goes blank: stop, put your pen down, take three slow breaths, and remind yourself you have sat two full mocks already — *you have done this before.* Then go back to the easiest question on the page and rebuild your rhythm from there.

# Final Pre-Exam Checklist

Tick each one off before you leave the house.

::: tip Documents & logistics
- Admission letter / test invitation
- National ID and any other required IDs
- Two working pens (and a pencil if allowed)
- Confirmed venue address and reporting time
- Planned route, leaving early
:::

::: note Mindset
- I have completed the 14-Day Sprint
- I have sat two full timed mocks and improved
- I know my plan: easy marks first, skip and return, stay calm
- I have eaten, slept, and arrived early
- I trust my preparation
:::

You prepared properly. You have a plan. You have practised under timed conditions and watched your own score climb. Most people in that room did none of this.

Walk in calm. Work the plan. **Get it. Read it. Pass it.**
